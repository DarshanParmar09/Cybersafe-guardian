🧱 Email Phishing Prevention Guide

Master these habits to make yourself phishing-proof

🔎
VERIFY THE SENDER

Always check the full email address — not just the display name. Attackers use look-alike domains like paypa1.com or amaz0n-support.net.

🔗
HOVER BEFORE YOU CLICK

Hover over every link to preview the real destination URL in your browser's status bar. If the URL doesn't match the claimed site, do not click.

🚫
NEVER SHARE CREDENTIALS

Legitimate companies never ask for passwords, OTPs, PINs, or credit card numbers via email. If an email asks for this, it's phishing — 100%.

⏱️
IGNORE URGENCY TACTICS

Phrases like "Act within 24 hours!", "Account will be deleted!", or "Immediate action required!" are designed to bypass rational thinking. Slow down and verify.

📎
BEWARE OF ATTACHMENTS

Never open attachments from unknown senders — especially .exe, .zip, .docm, .pdf files from unexpected emails. They may contain malware or ransomware.

🔐
ENABLE MFA EVERYWHERE

Multi-Factor Authentication ensures that even if attackers steal your password via phishing, they still can't access your account without the second factor.

🛡️
USE EMAIL FILTERS

Enable spam filters and email security gateways. Services like Google Workspace and Microsoft 365 have built-in anti-phishing protection that blocks most threats automatically.

📢
REPORT PHISHING EMAILS

In India, report phishing to report@phishing.gov.in or cybercrime.gov.in. Reporting helps authorities take down phishing infrastructure quickly.

🧠
THE GOLDEN RULE

When in doubt — don't click, don't reply, don't open. Contact the supposed sender directly through their official website or phone number to verify any email request.

🚩 TOP PHISHING RED FLAGS — INSTANT CHECKLIST
▹Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")
▹Mismatched sender & reply-to address
▹Spelling/grammar mistakes throughout
▹Suspicious shortened URL (bit.ly, tinyurl)
▹Request for credentials or OTP via email
▹Threats of account suspension / legal action
▹Prize / lottery / unexpected reward claims
▹Unexpected attachments from unknown senders
▹HTTP links (non-HTTPS) in emails
▹Urgency / extremely short deadlines
▹Too-good-to-be-true offers or deals
▹Lookalike domain names (paypa1, amaz0n)
